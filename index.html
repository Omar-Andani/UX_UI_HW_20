Project

Dance is for Everyone

Summary

I was part of a team responsible for redesigning the Kaeja d'Dance website.
Our goal was to make the interface more appealing to its many user groups.

Responsibilities
  • User Research
  • Ideation
  • Wireframing
  • Site Architecture
  • Visual Design
Background

About Kaeja

Kaeja d’Dance is a non-profit organization that has created award-winning contemporary dance performances for stage, film, and communities for over 30 years. Whether you’re a dance enthusiast looking to get started or a seasoned veteran seeking a challenge, Kaeja makes dance inclusive for all.

Research

The Problem at Hand

Through conversations with our target audience, we were able to determine what their expectations were when visiting a dance website. Our goal was to try and get into their minds and learn what they cared about. As we reviewed our notes, it became evident that the current Kaeja website wasn’t meeting its users’ expectations. It lacked a clear message as to the purpose of the organization, resulting in difficulty in users being able to access key information.

The site also lacked any sort of visual branding that one would expect when visiting a website of this nature; it was neither dramatic nor elegant.

Thus, we were left wondering:

How Might We improve the interface and information architecture of the site in order to inspire dance enthusiasts to spread their passion for dance to others?

Defining the User Experience

We saw Kaeja as more than just a dance company. Their community involvement and mission to make dance accessible to all resonates with countless individuals - many of whom have no previous dance experience. Once our persona was defined as a result of our interviews and secondary research we were then able to proceed in ideating the best possible ways for users to accomplish their task.

Due to time constraints, I focused on designing a specific user journey which led to an event called “Porch View Dancing (PVD),” identified through our interviews as Kaeja’s most popular event.

Making Information Accessible

To better connect users with the information they need, we conducted a moderated card sorting activity to better understand how the navigation should be improved. One of the key insights in our discovery process was learning that two user groups, dance enthusiasts and donors have specific needs when visiting the Kaeja website. Enthusiasts are looking for a section providing them with information on how they can start dancing and donors are looking for a section containing a repository of previous work. Both groups were explicitly addressed.

adding visual design

Finding a Style

After designing a new navigation then sketching a low-fidelity mockup, we were then able to develop and test our mid-fidelity prototype. Our user feedback was mostly concerned with cross-platform consistency and having too much text on the mobile version. Upon iteration, we then proceeded to add visual design elements to the site. To bring focus to our content and replicate the feeling of being in a theatre, where all the focus is on performance, we went with a dark theme maintained throughout.

Iterate and finalize

Test...and test some more

After testing the first version of our high-fidelity mockup and garnering feedback from our users, we proceeded to make a Version B for both mobile and desktop. Notable differences in Version B included an all uppercase letter navigation, modified button colours and transparency. Based on our A/B test results, the final design incorporates elements from both Version A and Version B.

contrast is key

The ‘Learn More' button was modified in Version B in order to provide a more minimalist look and added contrast for the user. It also proved to be more successful during our user tests. The same can be said for the arrows overtop the image. Lastly, the bottom navigation. Initially (for Version A), we thought it would be a good idea to bring attention to the “Donate” button which is why it was designed with a solid fill, but our users felt that this made it look like it was being selected, which caused confusion. As a result, we modified to include additional contrast, based on user preference as noted above.

legibility

We also went with an all uppercase global and local navigation for Version B in an attempt to improve readability. What we discovered was that while all uppercase navigation proved effective for shorter strings of text (i.e. two words or less), users found it difficult to read text that was any longer. Our final design (for both desktop and mobile), includes all uppercase for the global navigation but the subsequent local navigation remains lowercase.

Project Status

Kaeja d’Dance was created for a school project in collaboration with Kaeja’s Outreach Manager. We have presented our final designs to the stakeholder and will hopefully be implementing our designs just in time for Kaeja’s 30th anniversary, set to occur at the beginning of next year.
